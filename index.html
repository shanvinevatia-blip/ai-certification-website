◆ The case for AI literacy

Why this program exists

AI is not going away. The question is whether the Purdue community uses it in ways that strengthen human capability — or in ways that quietly erode it.

Our point of view

The rise of generative AI has created a silent crisis on college campuses. Students who once wrestled with difficult questions, constructed original arguments, and struggled productively toward creative solutions are increasingly outsourcing that struggle to a chatbot. The result isn't just academic dishonesty — it's a measurable erosion of the critical thinking, problem-solving depth, and creative confidence that a Purdue education is designed to build.

At the same time, refusing to engage with AI is not the answer. Professionals who cannot work alongside AI will be left behind. Researchers who don't understand AI limitations will misinterpret AI-assisted results. The workforce is changing — and Purdue has a responsibility to prepare its community for that reality.

This program takes a different position: AI literacy is not about restriction — it's about intention. When you understand how AI works, what it can and can't do, and what you risk when you hand your thinking to it, you can make deliberate, informed choices. That's what this certification teaches. Not rules. Understanding.

The problem with unreflective AI use

When students use AI to generate answers rather than work through problems, they skip the cognitive struggle that builds lasting understanding. Research on learning science is clear: productive difficulty is not a bug — it's the mechanism by which knowledge becomes durable. AI short-circuits that process when used without intention.

The problem with banning AI entirely

Blanket AI bans misrepresent how the technology works and leave students unprepared. Graduates who have never learned to use AI effectively will face significant disadvantages. Every industry from healthcare to engineering to law is integrating AI tools. Literacy means knowing how to use them — not just knowing they exist.

What AI literacy actually changes

Students who complete this certification make more deliberate choices about when to use AI and when not to. They are better at catching errors in AI output. They understand what data they're sharing. They know Purdue's actual policy — not rumors. And they can articulate to employers exactly how they used AI.

Why annual renewal matters

The AI landscape in 2026 looks nothing like 2023. Models are significantly more capable, more integrated into software, and more present in academic workflows. A certification earned once three years ago doesn't reflect current reality. Annual renewal ensures everyone stays current with evolving tools, updated Purdue policy, and emerging best practices.

Lesson 1 of 5 · Foundations
What is AI, really?
⏱ 20 minutes📖 Reading + quiz

Artificial intelligence is not magic, and it's not a sentient mind. It's a statistical system trained to recognize patterns in data. Understanding this — really understanding it — changes how you interact with AI tools and how much you trust their output.

How a language model actually works

A Large Language Model (LLM) like ChatGPT or Claude is trained on hundreds of billions of words from the internet, books, and code. Through this training, it learns to predict: "given this sequence of words, what word is most likely to come next?" That's it. It does this billions of times, adjusting its internal parameters to improve prediction.

This is why LLMs can write fluently on almost any topic — but also why they have no mechanism for checking whether what they say is true. They optimize for plausibility, not accuracy.

The hallucination problem: Because LLMs predict likely text rather than retrieve verified facts, they regularly generate statements that sound authoritative but are completely fabricated. This includes fake citations, wrong statistics, and confident descriptions of things that don't exist. Always verify claims from AI before using them.

Types of AI you'll encounter at Purdue

  • 💬Generative AI — creates text, images, code, or audio (ChatGPT, Claude, DALL-E, GitHub Copilot)
  • 🔍Discriminative AI — classifies or detects patterns (plagiarism detectors, medical imaging, fraud alerts)
  • 📊Predictive AI — forecasts outcomes based on patterns (recommendation engines, demand forecasting)
  • 🎮Reinforcement learning — learns by trial and reward (robotics, game-playing agents)

What AI cannot do

  • ❌Verify whether its output is factually correct
  • ❌Know what happened after its training data cutoff date
  • ❌Understand context the way a human reader does
  • ❌Replace the judgment, creativity, and accountability that make your work yours

Lesson 2 of 5 · Policy
Purdue's AI policy — what it actually says
⏱ 15 minutes📋 Policy + quiz

Purdue published its first official AI policy (Volume VII.A.5) on November 1, 2025. Before this, there were guidelines. Now there are rules. Here's what they mean for you.

The core principle

Responsible use of Generative AI and Large Language Models is required of all Purdue community members. AI tools and any university data entered into them are classified as IT Resources under Purdue's Acceptable Use policy. This applies to everyone — students, faculty, staff, contractors — with no exceptions.

How classroom policy works

  • 📄Your syllabus is the authoritative source — each instructor sets their own AI policy
  • ⚠️If the syllabus says nothing about AI, ask your instructor before using it for any assessed work
  • 🔒Consequences for unauthorized use must be stated in the syllabus

Purdue's approved tool: Microsoft Copilot with Data Protection is available to all Purdue students. It does not save chat history or use it for model training. Use this for any work involving university data — not consumer-grade ChatGPT or Gemini.

The Honor Pledge and AI

Purdue's Honor Pledge applies fully to AI use. Submitting AI-generated work as your own without authorization is a breach. The pledge is about intellectual honesty — and that principle doesn't change because a tool can write for you.

AI as a graduation requirement

Starting 2025–26, Purdue requires demonstrated AI working competency for graduation across all programs. No extra credits are needed — it is embedded in existing coursework. This certification fulfills that requirement.

Lesson 3 of 5 · Practice
Responsible use in practice
⏱ 20 minutes📝 Scenarios + quiz

Understanding policy is one thing. Knowing how to apply it in the messy reality of deadlines, complex assignments, and powerful AI tools is another. This lesson focuses on practical judgment.

✅ Things AI genuinely helps with

  • ✓Explaining a concept from class in a different way when you're stuck
  • ✓Brainstorming ideas or angles before you start writing
  • ✓Reviewing your grammar and sentence structure — you decide what to keep
  • ✓Generating example code to understand a pattern — then writing your own
  • ✓Asking it to critique your argument and find weaknesses in your reasoning

❌ What crosses the line

  • ✗Submitting AI-generated text as your original work without authorization
  • ✗Using AI to complete problem sets, exams, or quizzes you're meant to solve
  • ✗Paraphrasing AI output to disguise its origin in an unauthorized context
  • ✗Uploading your professor's slides, notes, or copyrighted course materials to AI tools

The disclosure rule: When in doubt, disclose. If you used AI in any part of your process and your syllabus requires disclosure, say so briefly: "I used ChatGPT to brainstorm an outline for this paper, then wrote all sections myself." That's honest. That's what academic integrity looks like.

How to cite AI (APA 7th edition)

OpenAI. (2025). ChatGPT (April 2025 version) [Large language model]. https://chat.openai.com — In-text: (OpenAI, 2025). Always include a brief author's note describing how you used it.

Lesson 4 of 5 · Skills
Critical thinking with AI
⏱ 20 minutes🧠 Skills + quiz

The most important AI skill isn't knowing how to prompt. It's knowing when to question. AI produces confident, polished, authoritative-sounding text — even when it's completely wrong. Critical AI literacy means developing a habit of skepticism alongside a habit of use.

A checklist before you trust AI output

  • 🔍Can you find this claim in a primary source? If not, don't use it.
  • 🔍Does the AI cite a specific source? Go look it up — citations are frequently fabricated.
  • 🔍Is the date important? AI has a knowledge cutoff and may be out of date.
  • 🔍Did the answer arrive too easily? If a hard question got a slick answer, probe it.
  • 🔍Have you asked a follow-up or challenged the reasoning? Try: "What are the strongest objections to this?"

The bias problem

AI models are trained on human-generated text — which reflects human biases, historical inequities, and dominant cultural perspectives. AI output can underrepresent certain groups and produce stereotyped characterizations. Be especially cautious when AI describes people, cultures, history, or contested topics.

Prompting for better thinking

  • 💡"What are the strongest counterarguments to [my thesis]?"
  • 💡"What assumptions am I making in this argument?"
  • 💡"Explain this step by step, then give me three questions to test my understanding."
  • 💡"What are the limitations of this approach?"

The two-source rule: For any factual claim from AI that you plan to use in coursework or professional work, require yourself to find at least two independent, non-AI sources that confirm it. This habit will catch more errors than any AI detector.

Lesson 5 of 5 · Protection
Privacy, data & your rights
⏱ 15 minutes🔒 Privacy + final quiz

Every time you type into a consumer AI tool, data leaves your device. Understanding what happens to that data — and what you're legally and ethically obligated to protect — is a core part of AI literacy.

What you must NEVER enter into consumer AI

  • 🚫Student records, grades, PUIDs, or any FERPA-protected information
  • 🚫Unpublished research data or proprietary lab findings
  • 🚫Social Security numbers, health records, or financial data
  • 🚫Copyrighted course materials — professor's slides, textbook passages
  • 🚫IRB-protected human subjects data or identifiable participant information

Your safe option: Purdue's 2024 agreement with Microsoft provides all students access to Microsoft Copilot with Data Protection. This version does not save chat history and does not use your data for model training. Use it for any task involving university information.

Copyright and AI

Your professors own the copyright to how they express and present course material — including their specific slides, lecture notes, and explanatory documents. Uploading these to a third-party AI tool is both a Purdue policy violation and a potential copyright infringement.

About the certification
The core course is recommended for all members of the Purdue community. For students, completion satisfies the AI working competency graduation requirement introduced for the 2025–26 academic year. Faculty, staff, and researchers are strongly encouraged to complete it, and some departments may require it. There is no cost to complete the program.
The core course takes approximately 90 minutes to complete. It consists of five lessons with reading content and a short quiz at the end of each. You can stop and resume at any time — your progress is saved. Specialized track courses add approximately 45 minutes each.
AI tools, Purdue policy, and best practices change significantly from year to year. A certification earned in 2023 does not reflect the capabilities of 2026 models, Purdue's November 2025 policy update, or current industry expectations.
