Basics of AI & how LLMs work
Module 01 · Foundational · All tracks

What is artificial intelligence?

AI refers to software that can perform tasks typically requiring human intelligence — understanding language, recognizing patterns, making decisions. Modern AI is powered by machine learning: systems learn from data rather than being explicitly programmed.

A Large Language Model (LLM) like ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini is trained on hundreds of billions of words from the internet, books, and code. It learns to predict the most statistically probable next word — which is why it can write fluently, but also why it can state false things with complete confidence.

Types of AI you'll encounter

  • 💬Generative AI — creates text, images, code, audio (ChatGPT, DALL-E, Suno, GitHub Copilot)
  • 🔍Discriminative AI — classifies or detects (spam filters, fraud detection, medical imaging)
  • 🎮Reinforcement learning — learns through trial and reward (robotics, game agents, AlphaGo)
  • 📊Predictive AI — forecasts based on patterns (recommendation systems, weather models)

How an LLM is built (simplified)

  • 1Pre-training on massive text datasets — learns grammar, facts, reasoning patterns
  • 2Fine-tuning on curated instruction-following examples — learns to follow directions
  • 3RLHF (reinforcement learning from human feedback) — human raters improve safety and helpfulness
  • 4Deployment — API or consumer interface with guardrails and system prompts

Key limitations to know

  • ⚠️Hallucinations — the model generates plausible-sounding but factually wrong content
  • ⚠️Knowledge cutoff — models don't know events after their training data ends
  • ⚠️No memory — each conversation starts fresh unless memory tools are added
  • ⚠️Bias — training data reflects human biases; outputs can replicate or amplify them

Purdue AI policy (VII.A.5)
Module 02 · Students & faculty · Issued November 2025

Official policy — Volume VII.A.5 (November 1, 2025)

Responsible use of Generative AI and Large Language Models is essential to Purdue's mission. AI tools and any university data entered into them are classified as IT Resources and Information Assets, subject to Purdue's Acceptable Use policy. The policy applies to all students, faculty, staff, contractors, and any parties accessing university systems — no exclusions.

How classroom AI policy works

  • 🏫Authorization is set at the course level by the instructor — there is no single university-wide classroom rule
  • 📄Your syllabus must state what is authorized vs unauthorized AI use for that course
  • ✅If your syllabus is silent on AI, ask before using any AI tool for assessed work
  • ⚠️Consequences for unauthorized use must also be stated in the syllabus

Approved tools at Purdue

  • 🔒Microsoft Copilot with Data Protection — approved, does not store or train on your data
  • 🔒Other Purdue IT-vetted tools may be approved per department
  • ❌Consumer ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini — may store data; do not use with protected university information

Key obligations for students

  • 🔒Never enter FERPA data, SSNs, or unpublished research into unapproved tools
  • 🔒Data you enter into external tools may become the provider's training data
  • 🏆AI working competency is now a graduation requirement across all Purdue programs (2025–26)
  • 👑Purdue's Honor Pledge applies — commit to honesty and transparency in AI use

Key obligations for faculty

  • 📄Must clearly state AI policy in the course syllabus before the semester starts
  • 👀Must maintain a human in the loop for all AI-assisted grading or assessment
  • ⚠️AI detection tools (Turnitin) have high false-positive rates — must not be the sole basis for an integrity charge
  • 🔒Must not share FERPA-protected student content with third-party AI tools without approval

Dos & don'ts of AI use
Module 03 · All tracks · Practical guidelines

✓ Do

→Use AI to brainstorm, outline ideas, or explore a concept from a different angle
→Verify every AI-generated fact with a primary or peer-reviewed source
→Disclose AI use when your syllabus, employer, or journal requires it
→Cite AI tools according to APA, MLA, or your field's citation guide
→Use AI to improve your grammar and structure — but review every suggestion yourself
→Ask AI to explain a concept in a different way when you're stuck
→Use Purdue-approved tools for any task involving university data
→Treat AI like a very fast research assistant — useful but needs supervision

✗ Don't

→Submit AI-generated text as your own original writing without explicit authorization
→Upload professor's slides, textbook PDFs, or copyrighted course materials to AI tools
→Trust AI output without fact-checking — models hallucinate plausibly and often
→Enter FERPA data, student records, SSNs, or confidential research into consumer AI
→Use AI on exams or assessments where it has been explicitly prohibited
→Pass off AI-generated code as your own in programming courses without disclosure
→Assume professors can't tell — AI detection is just one of many signals instructors use
→Skip reading AI output before submitting — you are responsible for all content you turn in

Academic integrity & AI
Module 04 · Students & faculty · Critical knowledge

Purdue's academic integrity rule

Purdue's student conduct code prohibits using unauthorized materials that give an unfair advantage. Unauthorized AI use is treated like copying a previous student's work — it is an academic dishonesty violation subject to formal consequences.

What counts as a violation?

  • ⚠️Submitting AI-generated writing as original work when the course syllabus does not authorize it
  • ⚠️Using AI to generate code, answers, or solutions on an individual assignment
  • ⚠️Paraphrasing AI output to disguise its origin
  • ⚠️Using AI during a closed-book exam or online quiz where prohibited
  • ⚠️Paying for or sharing AI-generated work between students

Consequences

  • 📋Grade penalty — from a zero on the assignment to course failure
  • 📋Referral to the Office of Student Rights and Responsibilities
  • 📋Permanent record in the university's academic dishonesty database
  • 📋Possible suspension or academic dismissal for repeated violations

AI detection — what you need to know

Turnitin and similar tools have very high false-positive rates. Purdue guidance explicitly states that instructors must treat AI detection results with "extreme distrust" and must never use a positive result as the sole basis for an integrity charge.

Real scenario: CS course

A CS professor at Purdue now requires students to come to office hours and verbally explain their code. If you used AI to write it and can't explain how it works, that's a red flag — far more reliable than any detection tool. AI literacy includes being able to work alongside AI, not just copy from it.

The gray areas

  • ⚪Using AI to check grammar on your own writing — usually fine unless prohibited
  • ⚪Asking AI to explain a concept you're studying — generally encouraged
  • ⚪Using AI to generate a first draft that you then heavily rewrite — depends entirely on your syllabus
  • ⚪Citing AI-generated content as a source — not appropriate; AI is not a citable authority
True or false: If Turnitin flags your paper as AI-generated, Purdue policy says that alone is sufficient evidence to charge you with an academic integrity violation.

Privacy, data security & FERPA
Module 05 · All tracks · Data protection

Why this matters at a research university

When you type into a consumer AI tool, that data may be stored by the provider, used to retrain the model, or shared in ways you didn't intend. At Purdue — home to billions of dollars in federally funded research — data leaks can have serious legal, financial, and reputational consequences.

What NEVER to enter into consumer AI

  • 🚫Student records, grades, PUID numbers, or any FERPA-protected information
  • 🚫Unpublished research data, proprietary lab results, or grant-funded findings
  • 🚫Passwords, API keys, access credentials, or network configurations
  • 🚫Personally identifiable information — Social Security numbers, health records, financial data
  • 🚫Copyrighted course materials — lecture slides, textbook passages, professor's notes
  • 🚫IRB-protected human subjects data or identifiable participant information

Safe AI options at Purdue

  • ✅Microsoft Copilot with Data Protection — Purdue's 2024 deal with Microsoft ensures no chat storage, no training on your data
  • ✅Check the Purdue IT acceptable use list before using any new AI tool for university work
  • ✅Only use fully public or properly anonymized data with consumer tools

Copyright & AI

Your professors hold copyright to how they express and present course material — not to underlying facts, but to their specific slides, notes, and explanations. Uploading those to a third-party AI tool is a Purdue policy violation and potentially a copyright infringement.

For researchers specifically

Pre-publication data, IRB protocols, and federally funded research results are especially sensitive. Check with your PI and the Purdue Research Office before using any AI tool in your research workflow.

Critical thinking with AI
Module 06 · All tracks · Essential skill

Why critical thinking matters more with AI

AI produces text that sounds authoritative, polished, and certain — even when it's wrong. Studies show people are more likely to believe AI-generated content even when it contains errors. Critical AI literacy means knowing how to interrogate output, not just use it.

A checklist for evaluating AI output

  • 🔍Can you find the claim in a primary source? If not, treat it as unverified.
  • 🔍Does the AI cite a specific source? Go look it up — citations are often fabricated.
  • 🔍Is the date important? AI has a knowledge cutoff and may not know recent events.
  • 🔍Does the answer feel "too perfect"? AI optimizes for plausibility, not truth.
  • 🔍Have you asked a follow-up or challenged the answer? Probe it.

Recognizing AI bias

AI models are trained on human-generated text, which reflects human biases — cultural, historical, political, demographic. This means AI output can underrepresent certain groups, over-index on certain perspectives, or produce stereotyped characterizations.

  • ⚠️Ask the same question multiple ways — look for inconsistency in representation
  • ⚠️Be especially skeptical in social science, history, or policy contexts
  • ⚠️Don't let AI be the only voice in research on marginalized groups or contested topics

Practice: the "two-source rule"

For any factual claim from AI that you plan to use in coursework or a professional document, require yourself to find at least two independent non-AI sources that confirm it before including it.

Prompt engineering skills
Module 07 · All tracks · Practical skill

What is a prompt?

A prompt is the instruction you give an AI. The quality of your output depends heavily on how well you write your prompt. Vague prompts produce vague answers; specific, structured prompts produce useful, targeted results.

The anatomy of a good prompt

  • 🌟Role — tell the AI who it is: "You are an expert in environmental law..."
  • 🌟Task — be specific about exactly what you want done
  • 🌟Context — give relevant background: audience, purpose, constraints
  • 🌟Format — specify structure: "respond in bullet points," "keep it under 200 words"
  • 🌟Examples — show a sample of what good output looks like

Weak vs strong prompt

Weak: "Tell me about climate change." — Strong: "Explain the top 3 mechanisms by which climate change affects agricultural yields in the US Midwest, in language suitable for a non-science undergraduate. Include one data point per mechanism."

Advanced prompt techniques

  • 💡Chain-of-thought — add "think step by step" to improve reasoning accuracy on complex problems
  • 💡Few-shot — give 2–3 examples of input/output pairs before your actual request
  • 💡Negative constraints — tell AI what to avoid: "do not use jargon," "do not include speculation"
  • 💡Iteration — treat the first output as a draft; refine with follow-up prompts
  • 💡Persona prompting — "Act as a skeptical editor and find weaknesses in this argument"

Prompting for different roles

  • 🎓Students — "Explain this concept to me like I'm a curious sophomore, then quiz me on it"
  • 📚Professors — "Generate 5 discussion questions on this topic at different Bloom's taxonomy levels"
  • 💼Professionals — "Summarize this report into 3 executive bullet points and flag any risks"

AI & your career or research
Module 08 · Professional, researcher & faculty tracks

Why Purdue requires AI literacy to graduate

Starting 2025–26, Purdue requires demonstrated AI working competency for graduation across all programs. No extra credits are needed — it is embedded in existing coursework. This reflects the reality that virtually every career will involve AI tools within the next decade.

Skills that employers value

  • 💡Prompt engineering — writing effective, efficient instructions for AI systems
  • 💡Critical evaluation — knowing when to trust AI output and when to override it
  • 💡Workflow integration — embedding AI tools into real processes without creating new risks
  • 💡Responsible use — bias awareness, privacy compliance, and ethical deployment
  • 💡Human-AI collaboration — contributing the judgment, creativity, and accountability AI cannot

How AI is reshaping majors at Purdue

  • 💻CS / Engineering — GitHub Copilot is standard; professors now require verbal code walk-throughs
  • 💊Nursing / Health sciences — AI in diagnostics and documentation; clinical judgment remains irreplaceable
  • 📈Business / Economics — AI for data analysis, report drafting, market research, and financial modeling
  • 📝Humanities / Social sciences — AI as research assistant; original argument and human voice are the value-add
  • 🔬Research / Graduate — AI for literature review, data processing, code, and hypothesis generation

Certification on your resume

Your Purdue AI Literacy Certificate is a shareable digital credential. Career professionals can display it on LinkedIn. The annual renewal signals to employers that your AI skills are current — not from a one-time 2023 course.
